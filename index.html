Mass Spectrometry

Mass spectrometry is an analytical technique that can be used to identify unknown compounds, quantify known compounds, and determine the structure and chemical properties of molecules by measuring their mass to charge ratio (m/z). This technique has several applications and has revolutionized scientific fields such as proteomics, environmental science and forensics, allowing researchers to study complex molecules with precision [1-2].

Mass spectrometers are the key to mass spectrometry as it is used to analyze molecules; it consists of three parts: the ion source, where a small sample is ionized into cations by losing electrons; the mass analyzer, where ions are separated based on their mass-to-charge ratio; and the detector, which measures the separated ions and displays the results on a chart [6-9].

Before using a mass spectrometer, a sample must be prepared for ionization [2]. This preparation involves converting the sample into either a liquid or gaseous phase, typically through chromatography techniques. The two types of chromatography commonly used for this are gas chromatography and liquid chromatography.

After the sample is prepared, The first process of the mass spectrometer apparatus is the ionization of the sample. This is accomplished by using a heated filament that has a charge of around 70 eV and bombarding the molecules from the sample with high energy electrons, knocking out electrons from the molecule and creating positively charged ions. This is a common method of ionization called electron impact ionization (EI) [2-3].

After the sample is ionized, the positively charged ions are accelerated by an electric field, which ensures they all have equal kinetic energy. Since the force applied depends on the ion's charge, ions of the same charge gain the same kinetic energy, but their velocities differ depending on their mass [2].

Deflection occurs after acceleration, where the ions are passed through a magnetic field which will deflect cations based on their mass and charge. Mass and deflection are inversely proportional, while charge and deflection are directly proportional. This means that ions with lower mass will experience greater deflection in the apparatus. Similarly, ions with a higher positive charge will also be deflected more. As a result, the apparatus can determine the mass-to-charge ratio based on these deflection principles [2].

In the detection phase, the mass spectrometer detects positive ions while neutral ones are removed by a vacuum. The most common detector, an electron multiplier, uses a series of dynodes with increasing potentials. Electrons are released and attracted to subsequent dynodes when ions strike the initial dynode, starting a cascade that can magnify the signal up to a million times. The amplified signal gives a highly sensitive measurement of the ions detected [2].

Mass spectrometry generates data on the mass-to-charge ratio of ions in a compound, displayed on a graph called a mass spectrum. A mass spectrum is usually presented as a vertical bar graph, in which each bar represents an ion having a particular m/z ratio and the length of the bar represents the relative abundance of that ion in the sample. The mass spectrum is crucial because it visually represents the distribution of ions in a sample, allowing scientists to determine the molecular mass, composition, and structure of a compound [2].

Mass spectrometry is a highly versatile and powerful analytical technique with several advantages and a few disadvantages. Among its advantages, mass spectrometry is applicable in various fields such as pharmaceutical research, forensics, and the environment. It is a vital tool in the pharmaceutical industry, as laboratories use it to identify the molecular formulas of pharmaceutical drugs [11]. Additionally, mass spectrometry can determine whether a drug is safe for release, although the FDA requires multiple tests to ensure that drugs are both safe and effective. Overall, mass spectrometry plays a crucial role in ensuring the quality and safety of pharmaceutical products before they reach the market. In forensics, mass spectrometry quantifies compounds found at crime scenes. For instance, scientists can analyze fabric collected from a crime scene to identify and measure various substances. This powerful technique provides critical evidence that can link suspects to crime scenes or uncover important details about the events that took place. Mass spectrometry also has environmental advantages, as it can detect organic compounds and contaminants at very low concentrations, enabling efficient monitoring and regulation of pollutants [11].

However, there are disadvantages: mass spectrometers are expensive, requiring investment in equipment and maintenance. Mass spectrometers can range from 10,000 - 1,500,000 depending on the model [14]. Furthermore, maintaining precision and accuracy demands extensive work, ultimately being very time-consuming and resource-intensive. Finally, the operation of mass spectrometers also has environmental impacts, as they are significant energy consumers as they require a lot of power for components in the mass spectrometer, emitting greenhouse gasses [10-12].

Mass spectrometry can be improved in many ways. One way we could improve mass spectrometry is by using a new, powerful technology: machine learning. By combining MS with machine learning algorithms for airborne pathogen surveillance, we can potentially predict the emergence of new viruses before they occur, enhancing our ability to prevent and respond to future pandemics [5]. Furthermore, mass spectroscopy is an expensive technology that can be improved by being more affordable for lower end researchers, allowing them to contribute and make advancements in the world of mass spectroscopy. Another new improvement for mass spectrometry is a new development of trapped ion mobility (IM-MS) [4-5]. This technique combines mass spectrometry with ion mobility, which distinguishes ionized molecules based on their motion through a gas. This improvement could allow for the separation of molecules not only by mass, but also by size and shape, which would improve the analyzing of complex proteins in living cells. When used alongside chromatography and advanced software, IM-MS provides a powerful way to study and understand complex samples, such as those in proteomics [7].

Mass Spectrometry is very effective for its intended purposes such as identifying compounds in complex mixtures, analyzing the structure of molecules and detecting amounts of substances. However there are many options and techniques that are useful such as chromatography, which is an alternative for analyzing large volumes of samples that is cost effective. and emerging protein sequencing methods that offer direct sequencing capabilities with improved sensitivity [9]. These other options are good, but mass spectrometry is known for its powerful analytical tools and is the best option for analyzing molecular data in all fields.
